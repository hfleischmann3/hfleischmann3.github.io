About Me

I recently graduated from the University of Michigan with highest honors in mathematics and highest honors in computer science. My honors thesis advisor was Greg Bodwin. While I am broadly interested in theoretical computer science and combinatorics, I am particularly passionate about extremal problems in discrete geometry, graph theory, and applying theory to make a social impact. I will begin my Ph.D. in Computer Science at Carnegie Mellon University during Fall of 2024.

I have several current projects related to hardness of approximation of geometric problems and network design.

  • I am studying the hardness of approximation of the Steiner tree problem with Karthik C.S. and Surya Teja Gavva. This is an extension of my work at the DIMACS REU program during the summer of 2022.
  • In a similar vein, I am studying optimal Euclidean Steiner trees for high dimensional point configurations like the regular simplex. This is a collaboration with Karthik C.S. and several of his 2023 DIMACS REU students: Guillermo Gamboa, Josef Matějka, and Jakub Petr.
  • I am also working on the hardness of approximation of clustering problems with Karthik C.S., Rajesh Chitnis, and Karthik's other 2023 DIMACS REU students: Ashwin Padaki, Styopa Zharkov, and Kyrylo Karlov.
  • I am investigating constructing adjacency oracles for multiplicative distance spanners in sublinear time with Greg Bodwin.

During the summer of 2023, I was a graduate mentor at the 2023 DIMACS REU program, working with Karthik C.S. and his students on hardness of approximation of clustering objectives and constructing optimal Euclidean Steiner trees for the regular simplex.

During the 2021 and 2022 academic years, I worked on quantifying the fairness of the redistricting plans drawn in the new redistricting cycle. I began as a student researcher in LoG(M) , advised by Tim Ryan and Samuel Hansen. With LoG(M), I developed a Python script for quantifying redistricting fairness in Michigan and provided feedback to the Michigan Independent Citizens Redistricting Commission on their initial practice maps. Later I became a policy analyst for CLOSUP at the Gerald R. Ford School of Public Policy. As a policy analyst, I worked with Gregory Herschlag and Jonathan Mattingly, processing Michigan geographic shapefiles and election data to apply a modified version of the Multi-scale Merge-split Markov chain redistricting fairness algorithm. Alongside Jon X. Eguia, I also quantified the fairness of every redistricting plan in the 2022 cycle according to simpler measures, co-authoring the IPPSR Partisan Advantage Tracker and issuing an associated policy report.

In the summer of 2021, I participated in the SMALL REU program at Williams College. At SMALL I was part of Steven J. Miller's Number Theory and Probability Theory group, and I worked on four subprojects spanning discrete geometry, random matrix theory, additive combinatorics, and combinatorial game theory. The discrete geometry subproject was co-advised by Eyvindur A. Palsson and Charles Wolf.

During the summer of 2020, I participated (virtually) in the Extremal Graph Theory and Dynamical Systems REU at Rochester Institute of Technology. I studied the existence of efficient $(j,k)$-dominating functions on graphs with Brendan Rooney.
